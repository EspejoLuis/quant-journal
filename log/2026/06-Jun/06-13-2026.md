## 🔑 Key Points

- **$N(d_2) = \mathbb{P}^Q(S_T > K)$:** under GBM, $S_T = S_0 \cdot e^{(r - q - \frac{\sigma^2}{2})T + \sigma\sqrt{T}\,Z}$. The event $S_T > K$ isolates $Z > -d_2$, so $P(Z > -d_2) = N(d_2)$. This is the BS analog of the indicator function in MC — not a condition, but a probability averaged across all paths.
- **$N(d_1)$ uses the stock measure:** for $E\!\left[e^{-rT} \cdot S_T \cdot \mathbf{1}_{S_T > K}\right]$, $S_T$ inside the expectation prevents factoring out $\mathbb{P}^Q(S_T > K)$. Changing numeraire to the stock measure (using $S_T / S_0$ as Radon-Nikodym derivative) gives $\mathbb{P}^S(S_T > K) = N(d_1)$. Same event, different drift.
- **$d_1$ vs $d_2$ — two independent dimensions:** call/put controls the sign ($N(d)$ for call, $N(-d)$ for put); asset-or-nothing vs cash-or-nothing controls which $d$ ($d_1$ for asset, $d_2$ for cash). Separating these into `dFactor` and `payoutFactor` eliminates nested switches.
- **Caching $d_1$ is pointless here:** caching helps when a value is computed multiple times. $d_1$ is computed once per `price()` call and used once — no repeated computation to eliminate.
