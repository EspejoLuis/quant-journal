## 🔑 Key Points

- **Digital put-call parity uses $+$ not $-$:** vanilla parity gives $C - P = S_0 e^{-qT} - K e^{-rT}$ because $\max(S_T - K, 0) - \max(K - S_T, 0) = S_T - K$ always. For digitals, $C - P$ changes sign depending on which side of $K$ we are. The clean identity is the sum: $C + P = S_0 e^{-qT}$ (asset-or-nothing) or $C + P = K e^{-rT}$ (cash-or-nothing), because the two indicators partition the real line.
- **Boundary convention — call uses $\geq$, put uses $<$:** under GBM, $P(S_T = K) = 0$ so it never matters for pricing. But with strict inequalities on both sides, parity fails at exactly $S_T = K$ (both fire 0). Convention: call claims the boundary so that $\mathbf{1}_{\text{call}} + \mathbf{1}_{\text{put}} = 1$ for all $S_T$.
- **Each product has its own parity structure:** put-call parity is not one universal formula — it is an algebraic identity derived from the specific payoff. The form ($C - P$ vs $C + P$, and what it equals) depends entirely on how call and put payoffs combine. Never copy vanilla parity to a new product without re-deriving it.
- **Chooser option decomposition:** at $T_c$, $\max(C, P) = C + \max(0, P - C)$. Substituting put-call parity gives $\max(0, K e^{-r(T-T_c)} - S_{T_c} e^{-q(T-T_c)})$ — a put payoff on $S_{T_c}$ with adjusted strike. Price $= C_{BS}(S_0, K, T) + e^{-qT_c} P_{BS}(S_0, K e^{-(r-q)(T-T_c)}, T_c)$. No simulation past $T_c$ needed.
